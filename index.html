Workflow

It all started as an university project. We were 6 members, so I decided to make a brainstorming with which everyone could feel listened and part of the final result. The brainstorming consisted on the following steps:
  1. Everyone listed a series of topics they were interested in.
  2. We, individually, created some videogame ideas out of combinations of those topics.
  3. There was a votation and we chose 3 games.
  4. For each of the videogames, we started linking ideas and developing a whole new videogame.
After that, we couldn't choose between the last three, so I made a 10 pager for each one. This way, they could know what videogame could come out from each idea. The chosen one was Miau Noodle Catfé (10 pager above)!
In that moment, we entered on production phase. I started writing the GDD and updating it. During that process, we had to solve a lot of problems.

Problems encountered

Some of the problems were:
  • Communication. We worked online, what can cause some communication problems. To avoid some issues between the programming and the art teams, I decided to make a room in the GDD for "Assets" section, where I listed all the art assets and SFX needed, each one with its naming notation. Programmers started to use placeholders and the art team just had to update those assets with the same name and everyone could continue working perfectly.
  • Screen Size. This game had to be playable in mobile devices as well. the art team wanted pixel-art, what made it even more difficult. I asked them to make a double-perspective. This way, the worktop could be seen from above and the clients, as if player were in front of them.
  • Prototyping. We were using Phaser for the first time and the programming team spent a lot of time on getting used to it, so I decided to make a prototype in Trello. I implemented a board with different columns: one for clients' orders, another one was the "coffee machine"... One member of the group started ordering coffees and pancakes, other one had to count everything and say when things were prepared, and the last one (me) was the player. After that, we changed roles. With this prototype, we got an idea of how much time the player needed and how oppressive it could be.

Don't Stop

Narrative 2D-Platform Game

Don't Stop is a narrative 2D-platform game about never giving up. In the game, the player has to discover how to use different gravity fields, boosts and more to make it to the end of the level as a metaphore of what the narrator is relating.
José Luis Murcia and I developed this game for an university project in which we got almost a perfect mark. Unfortunately, it had to be "only for mobile devices".

My contribution

  • I leaded brainstorming sessions.
  • I wrote the narrative script.
  • I worked as lead designer, from core mechanics and level design to UI, art and SFX decisions.
  • I worked as lead programmer, developing the different mechanics of the game.
  • I worked as lead artist, from concept to final art and animations.
  • One of the things I'm most proud of is the gravity system. I developed it as an asset, what made it really easy to start designing levels. We only had to drag and drop platforms, circles and it worked.

Level design

In this game, I wanted to talk about the experience of feeling sad, weak, without the energy of waking up everyday. It happened to me some time ago, and I was able to scape from that situation by not stopping. I had to wake up 5 days a week to go to university and I forced myself to talk, continue studying and meeting my friends..., and it worked!
I thought our mind is a really unknown, weird and non-sense place, as the space. I wanted the scenarios to be unreal and as open to metaphores as possible. That would help me to make a more accurate experience and an art work I was capable of. Furthermore, in space it is difficult to move, you have to impulse yourself constantly and things are really relative (even more if we take into account gravity), so it fitted perfectly! The main character actions are walking, jumping and impulsing in the air using energy. The main mechanics are different gravity fields, platforms, blinking platforms and propellers.
For each level, I made a scheme based on the curve of interest and the flow theory. I marked with a star those zones I wanted to be more difficult. Beacause of the narrative nature of the videogame, I wanted the player to master the mechanics before going on with more scripted zones where a "game over" would ruin the experience.
These are the 3 levels I designed for the game. Unfortunately, some of them were saved as really wide images, so this is the best way I found to show it. You can download it here!

Concept of the game

I started by researching some children books about space. I didn't want a realistic version. Actually, I wanted scenarios to become more and more different from it. But it was a good start to make the player feel okey while using different gravity fields. I could change it once she get used to that mechanic.

Workflow

It all started as an university project. We were 2 members, and my team mate didn't have much time and was sick, so the project was my responsability. We brainstormed together and finished up with a board of linked ideas we would like our game to implement. Then, I started researching, designing and prototyping mechanics, art and narrative.
After that, I implemented the gravity and cinematic-camera (zoom in and out, cinematic bars, etc.) systems.
After prototyping the character movement, I designed 3 different levels and implemented them. Finally, all we did was testing, updating and integrating art, voice lines, animations, etc.

Problems encountered

Some of the problems were:
  • For mobile devices. We had to make it work in a mobile device. The main problem here was the screen space. We decided not to have UI elements. The joystick is invisible and can be handled on the left side of the screen, while pressing the right side makes the character jump. To access the menu, players have to press the "go back" button of the device. Everything worked more or less fine, but people got confused and frustrated with the joystick. The fact that the player movement was slow didn't help. This will be solved in a future update with a visible and movable joystick.
  • Art. It was important for the game mood, but I was too bad at it. Neither of us had the talent or time to make a complex character with all the animations. This problem overlapped with some others: long distances to satisfy the "minimum gameplay time" restriction, how character would behave when switching between different gravity fields, how to make all the players to empathy with it... The solution was choosing a rectangle, adding a bounce behaviour and making all the ground not so flat. By doing this, we could have a character with few animations, easy to empathy with and with a friendly and organic move. All the emotions that a human face and gesticulation can provoke were replaced by the freedom of movement and mechanics that the rectangle gave to us. A person can not bounce constantly hitting the floor!

Miaumba

Competitive Local Multiplayer

Miaumba is a local multiplayer game about cats and toasts. The 2 players have to fight each other to be on the top when the toast hits the ground.
Mariam Baradi, Daniel Brenlla and me developed this game for a game jam. They made the art and I designed and programmed it.

My contribution

  • I leaded the brainstorming sessions.
  • I wrote the 10 pager document to make sure that everyone is on the same page.
  • I was the lead designer, focusing on gameplay and mechanics.
  • I was the lead programmer. Actually, I was the only one.
  • I made the Ground Pound Pound and Wind VFX.

This way, all those ideas can be concretized in an actually viable game and the team can decide whether they like it or not. It always requires more ideas to be added and some of them to be discarded, so this is a way of checking if they are ok with those changes I made.

    Workflow

    We all wanted a funny videogame with a good sense of humor. When this idea came up in the initial brainstorming session, we all liked it and had fun with it. There were some other good ideas, but this was the easiest for a game jam. At the end of the session we got the game idea about two cats fighting for staying on top of a falling toast colliding with obstacles.
    I thought the game needed a random component, so added random obstacles and power-ups. But there was another problem: the player couldn't turn the toast by her own. So I decided to implement a Ground Pound (called Miaumba) to add rotational force to the toast.
    I wrote the main aspects of the game in the 10 pager document and presented to them. Afterwards, we all started working. I had to prototype the core mechanic as fast as I could. It's difficult to change a lot of things in a game jam, but I wanted to make sure everything would work as expected.

    Problems encountered

    Some of the problems were:
    • Unity rigidbodies. I was expecting to get some help from Unity and its hierarchical system. The problem was that rigidbodies don't work right with parents that have a Rigidbody component as well. It was a problem because our two players had to move relative to the toast, which had to implement a complex physic behaviour I couldn't afford to do myself in a weekend. I tried a lot of options, but the solution was to implement cats movement myself setting their position on every frame and raycasting with vector calculations. When I got the movement more or less correctly, implemented game loop and scenes flow and sent it to my team.
    • Movement. The players rotate constantly, so what was right a second ago, maybe means left right now. I implemented a system in which the movement is relative to the camera, no matter how the cat is positioned. In order to avoid unexpected changes of directions, I detected when the toast was flipped and did not invert the controls of each player while they were holding the move right/left button.
    • Who moves the toast? No one was moving the toast. This provoked a weird static feeling. I thought it would be funny if the players could move it if they hold the move button when already reached the border. One of my main goals were to create as many points of conflicts as possible, so this was perfect for both things. We implemented a wind mechanic as well to give a more dynamic feeling.
    • It was too fast. The players could not react to obstacles or power ups, so I decided to warn them when something is coming. We also distinct between different power-ups so the players can decide wheter if they push the toast in order to get close or to avoid it.

Each Of Our Stories

Walking-simulator game

Each of Our Stories is a a Walking-simulator about social situation during COVID-19. It was made for a game jam where the theme was "portrait of the current situation".
In this game, the player is a postwoman that has to deliver letters at night. She is curious and the letters, somehow, are open, so she can read them. With every letter the player hands in, a visual representation of it appears in the town. Even thought it is a pretty calm place, the player is not alone, she got a motorbike with a cool radio to play songs and a camera. The game ends when the player takes 12 photos.
What I pretended with this videogame is to talk about some issues I cared about during the global pandemic we all suffered. Topics like nursing homes, students, elder marriages and lost are trated in the game. Once I put these topics on the table, I wanted to know what the players think about them and there's where the camera takes place.

My contribution

  • I made this game by my own. This means I had to use the time effciently and, Unfortunately, that I don't have design documentation about the videogame.
  • I was the lead designer, focusing on the experience.
  • I was the lead programmer. I made a solid camera system and a good-enough motorbike movement.
  • I integrated some assets that made it easier to construct and connect streets.

Workflow

I wanted to make a game I care about. I was influenced by Night Swimming, a videogame by Ludipe. I like this designer and he has helped me a lot everytime we have talked.
During COVID-19 worst moments, I thought a lot in some problems I felt nobody was talking about. So, at first, my only goal was to treat them in the game. But, at that time, I was starting to learn photography. Photography is a way of interprating things. When you make a photo, what it is actually portraid is your thoughts, feelings and point of view. As I was interested on people feelings about the topics I cared about, I decided to introduce a camera and make it the main mechanic. Who would say that some weeks later, a mobile company would be interested on my "motorbike game".
One of the things I started with was the scene. I wanted a procedural one, but it was too much work for the time I had. So I integrated a street asset with a buildings one, and made prefabs out of both. This was a problem, I could not manage the result I wanted. Even though I know how to model and animate, I didn't have enough time. After that, I started "production phase".

Problems encountered

The main problem was:
  • Camera mechanic. I wanted the camera to save the photos, so people could send them to me. I wanted them to be displayed as a texture to make a nice animation each time a photo is taken and show them all in the credits section. I still think there must be a way of saving real-time renders in a structure in Unity, but I ended up producing a temporal texture for the animation, saving the image locally and loading it in the credits section.

Actually, I had many problems, like performance issues, collision bugs, horrible controls, etc... Many of them I didn't have time to solve. But, even with those issues, this game is the most personal and least recent one and I had to include it in this portfolio. The feeling it provokes and the feedback I have received are really special to me. Probably the least pulished but the best videogame I've made so far.
