Miau Noodle Catfé

  • Time Management Game
    • This game is an university project about developing a videogame that could work in web and mobile markets.
    I was the lead designer and presented 3 different options to my team and we chose this one: a restaurant management game.
    Our professors decided to make a kind of competition between teams, and we won both jury (professors) and audience award!

My contribution

  • I worked as lead designer overseeing all the aspects of the game's design, from monetization to core mechanics.
  • I leaded the different brainstorming sessions.
  • Developing a presentation (10 pager) explaining all the main aspects of the videogame.
  • I worked closely with programmers and artists, listened to their needs and adapted the design.
  • Writing and updating the Game Design Document.
  • I prototyped the videogame workflow and main dynamics.

10 PAGER

When working with not-so-small teams, I like to make a kind of presentation which summarizes all the aspects of game design after the initial brainstorming sessions. This way, all those ideas can be concretized in an actually viable game and the team can decide whether they like it or not. It always requires more ideas to be added and some of them to be discarded, so this is a way of checking if they are ok with those changes I made.

GDD

This is the GDD. Unfortunately, I had to include some parts to make professors' correction easier.

Workflow

It all started as an university project. We were 6 members, so I decided to make a brainstorming with which everyone could feel listened and part of the final result. The brainstorming consisted on the following steps:
  1. Everyone listed a series of topics they were interested in.
  2. We, individually, created some videogame ideas out of combinations of those topics.
  3. There was a votation and we chose 3 games.
  4. For each of the videogames, we started linking ideas and developing a whole new videogame.
After that, we couldn't choose between the last three, so I made a 10 pager for each one. This way, they could know what videogame could come out from each idea. The chosen one was Miau Noodle Catfé (10 pager above)!
In that moment, we entered on production phase. I started writing the GDD and updating it. During that process, we had to solve a lot of problems.

Problems encountered

Some of the problems were:
  • Communication. We worked online, what can cause some communication problems. To avoid some issues between the programming and the art teams, I decided to make a room in the GDD for "Assets" section, where I listed all the art assets and SFX needed, each one with its naming notation. Programmers started to use placeholders and the art team just had to update those assets with the same name and everyone could continue working perfectly.
  • Screen Size. This game had to be playable in mobile devices as well. the art team wanted pixel-art, what made it even more difficult. I asked them to make a double-perspective. This way, the worktop could be seen from above and the clients, as if player were in front of them.
  • Prototyping. We were using Phaser for the first time and the programming team spent a lot of time on getting used to it, so I decided to make a prototype in Trello. I implemented a board with different columns: one for clients' orders, another one was the "coffee machine"... One member of the group started ordering coffees and pancakes, other one had to count everything and say when things were prepared, and the last one (me) was the player. After that, we changed roles. With this prototype, we got an idea of how much time the player needed and how oppressive it could be.

Don't Stop

Narrative 2D-Platform Game

Don't Stop is a narrative 2D-platform game about never giving up. In the game, the player has to discover how to use different gravity fields, boosts and more to make it to the end of the level as a metaphore of what the narrator is relating.
José Luis Murcia and I developed this game for an university project in which we got almost a perfect mark. Unfortunately, it had to be "only for mobile devices".

My contribution

  • I leaded brainstorming sessions.
  • I wrote the narrative script.
  • I worked as lead designer, from core mechanics and level design to UI, art and SFX decisions.
  • I worked as lead programmer, developing the different mechanics of the game.
  • I worked as lead artist, from concept to final art and animations.
  • One of the things I'm most proud of is the gravity system. I developed it as an asset, what made it really easy to start designing levels. We only had to drag and drop platforms, circles and it worked.

Level design

In this game, I wanted to talk about the experience of feeling sad, weak, without the energy of waking up everyday. It happened to me some time ago, and I was able to scape from that situation by not stopping. I had to wake up 5 days a week to go to university and I forced myself to talk, continue studying and meeting my friends..., and it worked!
I thought our mind is a really unknown, weird and non-sense place, as the space. I wanted the scenarios to be unreal and as open to metaphores as possible. That would help me to make a more accurate experience and an art work I was capable of. Furthermore, in space it is difficult to move, you have to impulse yourself constantly and things are really relative (even more if we take into account gravity), so it fitted perfectly! The main character actions are walking, jumping and impulsing in the air using energy. The main mechanics are different gravity fields, platforms, blinking platforms and propellers.
For each level, I made a scheme based on the curve of interest and the flow theory. I marked with a star those zones I wanted to be more difficult. Beacause of the narrative nature of the videogame, I wanted the player to master the mechanics before going on with more scripted zones where a "game over" would ruin the experience.

But it was a good start to make the player feel okey while using different gravity fields. I could change it once she get used to that mechanic.

Workflow

It all started as an university project. We were 2 members, and my team mate didn't have much time and was sick, so the project was my responsability. We brainstormed together and finished up with a board of linked ideas we would like our game to implement. Then, I sarted researching, designing and prototyping mechanics, art and narrative.
After that, I implemented the gravity and cinematic-camera (zoom in and out, cinematic bars, etc.) systems.
After prototyping the character movement, I designed 3 different levels and implemented them. Finally, all we did was testing, updating and integrating art, voice lines, animations, etc.

Problems encountered

Some of the problems were:
  • For mobile devices. We had to make it work in a mobile device. The main problem here was the screen space. We decided not to have UI elements. The joystick is invisible and can be handled on the left side of the screen, while pressing the right side makes the character jump. To access the menu, players have to press the "go back" button of the device. Everything worked more or less fine, but people got confused and frustrated with the joystick. The fact that the player movement was slow didn't help. This will be solved in a future update with a visible and movable joystick.
  • Art. It was important for the game mood, but I was too bad at it. Neither of us had the talent or time to make a complex character with all the animations. This problem overlapped with some others: long distances to satisfy the "minimum gameplay time" restriction, how character would behave when switching between different gravity fields, how to make all the players to empathy with it... The solution was choosing a rectangle, adding a bounce behaviour and making all the ground not so flat. By doing this, we could have a character with few animations, easy to empathy with and with a friendly and organic move. All the emotions that a human face and gesticulation can provoke were replaced by the freedom of movement and mechanics that the rectangle gave to us. A person can not bounce constantly hitting the floor!

Miaumba

Competitive Local Multiplayer

Miaumba is a local multiplayer game about cats and toasts. The 2 players have to fight each other to be on the top when the toast hits the ground.
Mariam Baradi, Daniel Brenlla and me developed this game for a game jam. They made the art and I designed and programmed it.

My contribution

  • I leaded brainstorming sessions.
  • I wrote the 10 pager document.
  • I was the lead designer..
  • I was the lead programmer.
  • I made the bomb VFX.
